# Preparing for AGI

**Artificial general intelligence (AGI)** refers to AI systems that can match or exceed human-level performance across a wide range of cognitive tasks, rather than excelling at just one. Australia may deploy increasingly capable systems across defence, critical infrastructure, finance, health and media without building or fully verifying those systems ourselves.

Most AI guidance focuses on near-term adoption, governance and compliance. This section complements that work with possible futures, Australian risk scenarios and the C·A·G·R framework for structured preparation across uncertain timelines.

!!! note "Scenarios for preparedness, not predictions"

    The scenarios, timelines and frameworks in this section are planning tools, not forecasts. They illustrate plausible risks to help Australian organisations prepare—not predict specific outcomes. Real events will differ in detail but may follow similar patterns.

**Time to explore:** 30–45 minutes | **Audience:** Government, business leaders, community organisations

---

## Why does Australia need AGI preparedness?

What happens when Australian organisations depend on systems developed overseas, cannot fully inspect them and still remain accountable for the consequences? That question matters before any particular definition of AGI is reached.

Australia is a small, advanced economy that imports most **frontier AI** while deploying it across finance, health, government and critical infrastructure. This creates a distinctive mix of exposure and influence. ([What is frontier AI?](concepts.md#what-is-frontier-ai))

**Important features of the Australian context:**

- **Geographic distance and long supply chains** can increase exposure to external disruption, while also creating incentives for domestic continuity planning
- **Concentrated sectors**, including banking, media and energy, can turn a provider or system failure into a broader sectoral problem
- **Five Eyes and regional partnerships** give Australia channels for technical cooperation, intelligence sharing and coordinated policy
- **Established regulators and public institutions** provide foundations for accountability, although capability and authority vary by sector
- **Universities and research organisations** can contribute to independent evaluation and safety research
- **Procurement, sectoral regulation and deployment decisions** give Australia practical influence over systems used here, even when their development happens elsewhere

This section addresses Australia's specific context—not the US, UK or EU.

## What strategic challenges does Australia face?

Australia is likely to deploy advanced AI systems that were developed overseas and cannot be fully verified by local users or regulators. This creates three connected strategic problems:

### The information problem: We can't verify what we import

Frontier AI providers assure us their systems are safe and aligned. But we face fundamental information asymmetries: we don't observe training processes, current techniques cannot reliably determine what systems are optimising for and systems may appear aligned in testing but pursue different goals in deployment ([deceptive alignment](concepts.md#what-is-ai-alignment)). Even well-intentioned providers cannot provide strong, independently verifiable guarantees of safety today—alignment verification remains an open technical challenge.

!!! info "Australia's AI Safety Institute: National evaluation capability"

    Australia's [**AI Safety Institute**](https://www.industry.gov.au/science-technology-and-innovation/technology/artificial-intelligence/ai-safety-institute) is operating within the Department of Industry, Science and Resources (as at July 2026). It analyses and tests emerging AI models and applications, supports regulators and agencies and contributes to international AI safety work. It strengthens Australia's independent technical capability, but does not replace the enforcement roles of sectoral regulators.

### The coordination problem: International cooperation is hard

Many protective measures work best with international cooperation, including compute governance, export controls and safety standards. Coordination nevertheless faces structural barriers: competitive dynamics can create pressure to deploy before safety validation, nations with frontier AI industries may have different incentives from importers and treaty processes can move slowly. Australia can contribute through Five Eyes partnerships and multilateral forums, but domestic plans should not depend on timely international agreement.

### Time as a constraint: Capability and governance move at different speeds

Some AI capabilities and deployment practices are changing faster than legislation, institutional expertise and assurance methods. The practical concern is not only [when AGI might arrive](timelines.md), but whether evaluation, governance and continuity arrangements can keep pace with the systems organisations choose to deploy.

**These problems justify early, proportionate preparation.** The [C·A·G·R Framework](framework/index.md) uses defence-in-depth because no single technical, institutional or operational safeguard is sufficient.

## Our approach: Containment · Alignment · Governance · Resilience

Advanced AI poses two parallel challenges: **safety failures** (systems that cause harm) and **governance gaps** (questions of control and accountability that persist even with safe systems). Both require attention, which is why we recommend Australian organisations structure AGI preparedness around **four interconnected pillars**:

- **[Containment](framework/containment.md)** – Preventing dangerous AI systems from being developed or escaping control
- **[Alignment](framework/alignment.md)** – Making AI systems reliably pursue intended goals and remain safe as capabilities scale
- **[Governance](framework/governance.md)** – Laws, institutions and coordination that shape how AI is developed and deployed
- **[Resilience](framework/resilience.md)** – Building capacity to withstand and recover from AI-related disruptions

These pillars form **layers of defence**. Containment aims to prevent dangerous systems from being created or deployed. Alignment seeks to make systems safer by design. Governance provides oversight and coordination. Resilience prepares Australia to withstand disruptions if other layers fail.

!!! info "C·A·G·R reflects current best practice, not complete solutions"

    Key limitations: we cannot reliably verify alignment (systems may appear safe in testing but behave differently once deployed), capability limits remain unclear and international coordination is lacking. This is why defence-in-depth matters—no single layer suffices.

## Common questions

??? question "Isn't this just science fiction? Why worry about AGI now?"

    **Why this is not only hypothetical:** Recommendation algorithms optimised for engagement can amplify polarisation. AI systems sometimes behave unexpectedly, diverging from intended goals. Content moderation exhibits biases designers did not anticipate. As capabilities increase, systems can gain autonomy and become harder to oversee. Techniques that align narrow systems may not scale to highly capable agents. (See [Concepts](concepts.md) for examples.)

    **Why prepare now:** Evaluation capability, governance arrangements and continuity planning take time to build. Starting before a high-impact deployment leaves organisations more options than responding after dependencies are established.

??? question "What can Australia realistically do? We don't build frontier AI."

    **Australia's practical leverage is over deployment:** Australia does not currently host a leading frontier-model developer. Existing laws, sectoral regulation, government procurement and critical-infrastructure obligations nevertheless shape how AI systems can be used here. The exact approval and enforcement pathway depends on the sector and use case. Domestic evaluation capability can help regulators and organisations assess provider claims rather than relying on assurances alone.

    **Where Australia can act:** Procurement, sectoral regulation, Five Eyes cooperation, universities and domestic evaluation capability give Australia several practical channels for influence. The strength of each channel depends on the system, sector and international context.

??? question "How does this relate to current AI governance?"

    **Most current AI governance addresses near-term risks** (bias, privacy, security, labour displacement) through transparency requirements, impact assessments and sector-specific regulation. This work is essential and forms the core of SafeAI-Aus.

    **Preparing for AGI addresses transformation risks** that may arise across a wide range of timelines: advanced systems in critical infrastructure, power concentration, [loss of control](concepts.md#what-is-loss-of-control) and societal disruption. These demand different tools: capability evaluation, containment strategies, resilience planning and [defence-in-depth](concepts.md#what-is-defence-in-depth) approaches.

    **Both matter.** Near-term governance builds institutional capacity; AGI preparation can improve readiness for advanced systems.

??? question "Should we slow down AI development?"

    **This is one of the most contentious questions in AI governance.** Arguments for slowing down: more time for safety research, less deployment under competitive pressure, room for societal adaptation. Arguments against: ceding advantage to competitors, forgoing genuine benefits (medical research, climate science), political infeasibility.

    A practical Australian debate could focus on coordinated, risk-based capability thresholds and evidence before high-risk deployment, rather than treating a blanket slowdown as the only option. Domestic evaluation capability would make that debate better informed. See the [C·A·G·R Framework](framework/index.md) for the trade-offs across containment, alignment, governance and resilience.
